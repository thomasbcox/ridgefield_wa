The city is actively planning for today and tomorrow. Its strategy involves saving water and building for the future.

Building for Tomorrow: New Water Projects

To meet future needs from growth, the city is building new water systems. These projects will help make sure Ridgefield has enough water for years to come.

Eastside Elevated Water Reservoir

This important project involves building a new large water tank on the east side of the city. It will hold about 2.5 million gallons of water. This new tank will help provide enough water for the next 15-20 years, especially for the eastern part of Ridgefield.

  • Expected Capacity: 2.5 Million Gallons
  • Designed to meet needs for 15-20 years
  • Design Phase: Until Fall 2025
  • Construction: Summer 2025 - Spring 2027
  • Future plans for a second tank on site

Ridgefield's Water Planning: A Balanced View

The idea that Ridgefield has planned well for its water systems, given its quick growth, has different viewpoints. Here are arguments for and against this idea.

Case Against the Thesis: Challenges to Effective Planning

  • Current Shortage & Early Rules: The city is in Stage 3 water saving rules early in the year. This suggests current water use is too high for the available supply.
  • Demand vs. Developed Supply: Peak summer water use (3.5 MGD) is more than what working wells can provide (2.5 MGD). This shows a gap.
  • Lower Aquifer Levels: Water levels underground are deeper than normal. This means current water use might be too much for the aquifer.
  • Fast Population Growth: Very quick growth makes it hard for water systems to expand fast enough to keep up.
  • Need for Backup Water: Relying on Clark Public Utilities for extra water suggests the city's own system is not always enough.

Case For the Thesis: Evidence of Good Planning

  • Long-Term Water Rights: The city has legal rights to much more water (5.0 MGD) than it uses. This shows foresight for future needs.
  • New Infrastructure: Building a large new Eastside Elevated Water Reservoir (2.5 MG) is a clear step to add more water capacity for the future.
  • Structured Conservation: A 5-stage plan to save water shows the city actively manages water use during different conditions.
  • Backup Supply Agreement: The deal with Clark Public Utilities provides a vital safety net for high demand or emergencies.
  • High Water Quality: Consistently meeting water quality standards shows strong daily management of the water system.

Backup Supply: Staying Prepared

Beyond its own wells, Ridgefield has a key backup plan: a deal with Clark Public Utilities (CPU). This lets the city buy extra water when needed, like during high demand, maintenance, or emergencies. This backup adds a vital layer of safety to the city's water supply.

Ensuring Water Quality

While the city manages water quantity, its water quality remains consistent with standards.

Meeting Water Quality Standards

Ridgefield's drinking water meets all state and federal safety rules. The city cleans its water with chlorine and special filters to remove minerals.

  • Voluntary chlorination is applied for disinfection.
  • Filters remove naturally occurring minerals to reduce hardness.
  • Regular tests are conducted for a wide range of potential contaminants.
  • Test results indicate no detection of regulated contaminants in the city's water.
  • A robust Cross-Connection Control Program is in place to prevent backflow into the system.

Water Hardness

Ridgefield's water from the Troutdale aquifer is naturally moderately hard. It averages 100 parts per million (ppm) of minerals and typically contains 55 ppm of silica. The city's filter plant helps reduce these mineral levels.

This means the current water challenge is about having *enough* water, not about its *safety*.
