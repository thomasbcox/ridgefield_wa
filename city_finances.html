Financing the Boom

An analysis of Ridgefield, Washington's high-stakes public spending strategy from 2019-2024.

The Scale of Growth

From 2019 to its 2023 peak, Ridgefield's population grew rapidly, but its city budget expanded at a far more explosive rate, signaling a fundamental shift in fiscal strategy.

The city's expenditure per capita has more than doubled, from $1,983 in 2019 to a peak of $5,916 in 2023.

Where is the Money Going?

The budget explosion is driven by a massive pivot towards capital projects, primarily for infrastructure. Spending on long-term projects has skyrocketed while operating funds for daily services have seen more modest growth.

Capital Budget Growth (2019-2024)

1,200%+

In 2019, capital projects were just 20% of the budget. By 2024, they accounted for nearly 60%.

The Engine of Debt: Tax Increment Financing (TIF)

To fund its ambitions, Ridgefield is relying heavily on two large TIF districts. This tool allows the city to borrow against future, speculative property tax growth. However, independent reviews show this strategy socializes significant financial risk, leaving taxpayers to cover shortfalls if development doesn't meet forecasts.

A Network of Influence?

Public records suggest a close relationship between the industries benefiting from public spending and organizations shaping local policy. It is common in American towns for organized business groups, like chambers of commerce or industry associations, to play an active role in shaping development policy. This pattern is not unique to Ridgefield.

Main Street Tax Credit Donors

Ridgefield Main Street is a non-profit organization with its own budget (approximately $130,000 in 2023) and board of directors, separate from the city. Through a state program, businesses can donate their tax dollars to the non-profit. Analysis shows donors are heavily concentrated in sectors poised to gain from city-funded infrastructure.

An Invitation for Transparency

In June 2024, the City Council approved a lease for its own offices with "Tri-Mountain Investors, LLC". Under Washington State law, the owners of a Limited Liability Company (LLC) are not required to be publicly disclosed. (The author of this analysis also owns an LLC that uses a registered agent for privacy.)

While the lease's value is not specified in public documents, such agreements typically represent a very small fraction of a city's total operating budget. The lack of public information about the LLC's owners is not evidence of wrongdoing. However, for the sake of greater public clarity, the City or the principals of the LLC may wish to voluntarily share more information about the arrangement.

This situation highlights an opportunity to enhance public trust through voluntary disclosure.

A Note on Financial Reporting

It is important to note that the City of Ridgefield's financial documents are presented with a high degree of professional clarity and organization. The city has consistently received the Distinguished Budget Presentation Award and the Certificate of Achievement for Excellence in Financial Reporting from the Government Finance Officers Association (GFOA). These awards indicate that the city's budget and financial reports adhere to nationally recognized standards for governmental accounting and transparency, making the information accessible for analysis.

How Does Ridgefield Compare?

Benchmarking against Black Diamond, another fast-growing WA city with a 2022 population of about 6,000, provides context for Ridgefield's financial strategy. The data shows Ridgefield's financial profile has a significantly higher emphasis on capital spending, though its overall spending per resident is more comparable.

While both cities are managing major growth, Ridgefield dedicates more than double the percentage of its budget to long-term infrastructure projects compared to its peer city. This highlights a different approach to financing development.

Inside the Operating Budget: A Look at Core Services

Breaking down the operating budget reveals how spending priorities for core services in Ridgefield compare to a representative model for a Washington city of similar size. This provides insight into how resources are allocated for day-to-day functions.

Ridgefield appears to allocate a larger portion of its operating budget to Public Works and Community Development compared to the representative model. Conversely, it allocates a smaller percentage to Public Safety. This may be because Ridgefield contracts for fire services with Clark-Cowlitz Fire Rescue, whereas many cities fund their own fire departments directly within their operating budget.

Ridgefield's Per-Capita Operating Spending Trend (2019-2024)

Looking at Ridgefield's own history shows that spending on day-to-day services has been rising along with its population. This chart shows the trend for the years where reliable data is available.

The data shows a steady increase in per-capita operating spending from 2019 to 2024. This indicates that while capital projects are the largest part of the budget, the city has also been increasing its budget for core services to keep pace with its growing community.

Conclusion: A High-Stakes Gamble

Ridgefield has embarked on an ambitious, debt-fueled strategy to catalyze commercial growth. It has socialized significant financial risk, betting that massive public investment will create private development valuable enough to pay for it all. If the bet fails, taxpayers could face decades of debt and a potential strain on essential public services.
